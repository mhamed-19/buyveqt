§ 01Strategy

Three bigger bets, in order of effort.

The heatmap was a swing. Round 3 needs three more, escalating from a one-component addition to a structural rethink to an entirely new section the site doesn’t have. Each bet below is argued, then mocked, then specified.

Underlying thesis

VEQT is unusual because it is boring on purpose — and contains the world. The site should make both halves legible: the discipline (one ticker, hold forever) and the contents (thirteen thousand companies across forty-seven countries). Round 1 nailed the discipline. Round 2 left the contents almost entirely undisclosed.

BET 01

The What You Own panel

Effort · Medium Impact · High

VEQT holds 13,500 companies. The site never shows you a single one. That’s the most interesting fact about the fund and it’s the most aggressively under-told story on the property.

This is the Round 3 equivalent of the heatmap: one ambitious component on the home page that gives a confident, opinionated visualization of an otherwise opaque thing. The mock at right shows it: a region treemap on the left (US, Canada, Developed-ex-US, EM), a top-10 list on the right with weights, and one number underneath — "the bottom 13,490 holdings together weigh less than Apple alone." That sentence, if true, sells the entire fund.

Crucially this is not a pie chart. It’s a treemap because pie charts can’t do 13k slices and a treemap with the long-tail collapsed into a single cell is the visual argument: the long tail looks small precisely because it is.

Build cost: a single HoldingsPanel.tsx with static JSON. The data doesn’t need a feed — Vanguard publishes it quarterly and you can hand-paste it.

BET 02

Rebuild /compare as a behavioural document

Effort · Medium-High Impact · High

"VEQT vs XEQT vs ZEQT" with five columns is a Wikipedia table. Every Canadian PFC blogger has the same one. The reason this comparison is interesting is not the MERs — they’re effectively identical — it’s how each fund actually behaved when something happened.

Replace the table with three vertical narrative columns, each anchored to a real event: March 2020 (covid), October 2022 (rate-hike trough), August 2024 (yen carry unwind). Show the drawdown, the recovery time, and one sentence each on why these funds diverged that week.

This is a structural rethink: the page becomes a piece of writing, not a spec sheet. The MER table can live below the fold for the people who came for that. The headline becomes “Three funds. Three moments. The differences you can’t see in a fact sheet.”

This is the page that converts a casual visitor into a believer. It’s also the page that buys the brand permission to use vermilion at all — right now the red is doing decoration; on this page it would be doing argument.

BET 03

A new section: VEQT through history

Effort · High Impact · Defining

VEQT was launched January 29, 2019. The site has zero coverage of the seven years since. The 90-day heatmap is a microscope; this is the telescope.

Build /history as a single, scrollable, year-by-year reading of the fund: a giant timeline that scrolls horizontally inside a sticky frame, with five anchors — 2019 launch · 2020 crash · 2022 rates · 2024 yen · today — and at each anchor a price, a drawdown, a paragraph of what was happening, and (the trick) "$10,000 invested at this moment is now worth…".

This is the section that turns the inception calculator on the home page into a real story. The calculator answers "what would I have made"; this section answers "what would I have lived through."

It is the most ambitious item on this page. It is also the one that, if it lands, single-handedly makes the site a destination instead of a fact sheet.

§ 02Teardowns

Three pages that are letting the home page down.

The home page does heavy lifting. The interior pages don’t. A user who clicks deeper currently gets less ambition, not more. These three are the most exposed.

/compare

Five-column ETF table that doesn’t earn its h1.

Replace
As built

A standard responsive table: Ticker · MER · AUM · Holdings · Inception. Headline says "the differences are not zero" — the table doesn’t prove it. MERs are 0.24/0.20/0.20. AUMs differ but who cares. Inception years are roughly the same.

The single most interesting comparable — regional tilt — is missing entirely. The CSS for it (.bs-tilt-bar) is in globals.css:1300, suggesting someone started.

Reads like a Wikipedia page. No reason to bookmark, share, or come back.

Proposed

Demote the spec table; promote the behavior. Lead with Bet 02 above — three event columns. The MER/AUM/Holdings table goes under a "If you’re here for the spec sheet…" heading at the bottom.

  • Hero — the three events (Bet 02), full-width.
  • Tilt comparison — stacked 100% bars, US/CA/Dev/EM, three rows.
  • Spec table — moved below the fold, condensed.
  • One verdict line — "they hold the same world, weighted differently."
/invest · calculators

The most generic page on the site.

Reframe
As built

Two calculators: "if I had invested $X in 2019…" and "if I invest $Y/month for Z years…". Inputs left, big number right. It’s correct. It’s also indistinguishable from every advisor’s site.

The result is presented as a single cumulative number. Useful for ego, useless for understanding. There’s no distribution, no worst case, no sequence-of-returns intuition.

Proposed

Reframe both calculators as "what actually happened to people who started in…". Same inputs, very different output: a fan chart of all rolling cohorts who started in different months, with the user’s scenario highlighted as one line in the cloud.

  • Cohort fan — 84 monthly cohorts since launch, plotted as light lines.
  • Your line — bold red, your specific start month.
  • Three numbers — median outcome, your outcome, worst-case cohort.
  • One sentence — "you started in March 2020. 71 of 84 cohorts did better than yours; 13 did worse."
/learn

Overshot the brief. Now scattered.

Refocus
As built

Filter rail · Editor’s Picks · Paths · grouped categories. It’s a CMS-shaped page with seven affordances and no path. The Round 2 plan asked for "a reading order in three parts" — what shipped is the opposite of that: every order, all at once.

The home page’s "From the archive · Three to start with" was supposed to feed into Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3. Neither end of that pipe got wired up.

Proposed

Cut the page in half. Lead with three named courses, three articles each, in order. The grouped/filtered archive lives below as "Everything else, by topic."

  • Course 1 — "What VEQT actually is" (3 reads).
  • Course 2 — "Why one fund and hold forever" (3 reads).
  • Course 3 — "What to do when it’s down" (3 reads).
  • Below — full archive, current grouping intact.
  • Home page — renders Course 1, three numbered cards. The promised pipe.
