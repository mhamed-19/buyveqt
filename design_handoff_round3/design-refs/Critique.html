House Verdict — Strong Buy, with revisions

A genuinely distinctive site, hiding a small identity crisis.

The broadsheet aesthetic is the best decision on the property — it's specific, opinionated, and gives the data room to mean something. But the older "card-editorial" hero, the scattered calculators, and an over-reliance on serif italics are softening the edge. Commit harder to the paper, prune the rest.

By the design desk  ·  Filed remotely, April 26

Concept 9/10 A daily paper for one ETF — instantly memorable.
Typography 8/10 Fraunces + Newsreader sing. Too many italics.

Section I — What works

The paper, when
it’s really a paper.

Most VEQT-adjacent sites look like brokerage dashboards in a trenchcoat. Yours doesn't, and that's the moat. The home page reads — and that's a verb that almost no finance site earns. The five things below are the ones to protect at all costs.

№ 01Keep

The broadsheet conceit is a genuinely original framing.

Cream paper, ink rules, a "Live Wire" stamp, "Letters" from the subreddit, "From the archive" — every piece of the page has been re-named in service of a single metaphor. That's editorial discipline most fintech never reaches.

"VEQT holds four Vanguard index ETFs… your job is not to sell." — homepage region intro

№ 02Keep

The Severity Meter is the single best thing you've built.

Frequency-weighted zone widths, a sentence keyed to the zone, an inline legend that doesn't fight the strip — this is real information design. It teaches behaviour ("a typical day moves ±X%") instead of just rendering a number.

Protect it Promote this above the fold on /learn and /compare too. It's a brand asset.

№ 03Keep

Region cards do work that pie charts cannot.

Ranking the four sleeves with a vermilion underline on the day's leader, surfacing the contribution in pp, and showing 30-day sparklines under each one is a small masterclass. The reader can verify the lead headline visually — that's trust, by construction.

№ 04Keep

The inception calculator is flirty, not boring.

Embedding the input inside the headline ("If you'd bought $10,000 of VEQT at launch") with the math updating live in the sidebar table is delightful. It's the right pattern for the brand — playful inside a serious frame.

№ 05Keep

The voice has actual taste.

"Past performance is a terrible predictor of future performance." "Three ETFs, one job: own the world." "The differences are smaller than Reddit will have you believe, but they are not zero." This is the rarest thing on a finance site: a writer.

№ 06Keep

Two type voices, not five — finally.

Fraunces (display) for headlines, Newsreader (text serif) for body, Outfit (sans) for labels and stamps. Three families, each with a clear job. Most editorial sites use double that.

The site is a finished newspaper trapped inside a half‑finished SaaS landing page.
— The verdict, in one sentence

Section II — What to sharpen

Where the
ink is running.

You have two design systems on the same property. The "broadsheet" tokens — paper, ink, stamp, rule — and the older "editorial-finance" tokens — card-editorial, brand red, rounded buttons, pill stats. They share a vocabulary but not a soul. The crit below is mostly: pick one, kill the other.

№ 01Cut or rebuild

The legacy HeroSection contradicts the masthead.

Pill-shaped stat tags, a rounded "Compare VEQT vs XEQT" button, animated count-ups, and a rotating verb ("understood / compared / chosen") — this is the SaaS playbook your homepage explicitly rejects. If a user lands on a page that still uses it, the brand whiplashes.

Action Audit every route. Anything still rendering HeroSection, card-editorial, or rounded brand buttons gets ported to broadsheet primitives or deleted.

№ 02Sharpen

Italic is a spice, not a sauce.

"came from." "vs." "slowly." "the field." "Pick the question." Almost every section header italicises one or two words. It was charming twice; by the eighth occurrence it reads like a tic. Reserve italics for one moment per page — the lead headline.

Replace half the italic words with a regular weight. The remaining italics will actually feel italic.

№ 03Sharpen

The masthead is doing five jobs at once.

Live-wire stamp, full date, theme toggle, hamburger, nameplate, ticker block, department rail, mobile drawer — all stacked vertically before any content. On a 1024-tall laptop the lead headline is below the fold. Real broadsheets compress the masthead because the news is the point.

Action Collapse the wire-state and date into one strip with the department rail. Move the ticker block into the lead, not next to the title. Should save 80–120px above the fold.

№ 04Sharpen

No clear answer to "what do I do now?"

The page tells me where today's move came from, what $10k at launch is worth, and what to read. It doesn't tell me whether to buy, hold, contribute, or rebalance. That's a feature for trustworthiness — but the call-to-action is missing entirely. A reader who's convinced has no place to go.

Action Add a single "Today's edition recommends" section — one sentence ("Hold. Contribute on schedule. Ignore the move.") plus one link to the most relevant Learn article based on severity zone.

№ 05Cut

Rotating words and count-up numbers are vestigial.

"VEQT, understood / compared / chosen." A 13,700 number that animates from zero. These are pre-broadsheet artifacts. They flatter the eye for one viewing and grate forever after.

Action Strip them. The serif headline can carry the page on its own.

№ 06Sharpen

The compare table is under-designed for what it claims.

Three rows, one "House Choice" stamp on VEQT, equal weight per cell — but the headline says "the differences are not zero." The table doesn't show the differences. MER is the same. AUM is a vanity stat. Holdings are nearly identical. The reader leaves without learning the actual gap (Canadian vs US tilt; bond exposure for VGRO).

Action Add a fourth column: "Tilt" — render a small horizontal stack-bar of US/CA/Intl/EM weights per ETF. That is the difference, drawn.

№ 07Sharpen

"Learn" feels like a blog index, not a curriculum.

Twenty-five MDX articles, nine learning paths, a filter rail, editor's picks — there's enough content for a real syllabus, but the home page surfaces only "three to start with." A new reader can't tell whether they're at article one of one or article one of twenty-five.

Action On the home page, replace "three to start with" with one prescribed reading order: Start here → Read next → Then this. Demote the rest to /learn.

№ 08Sharpen

The vermilion stamp is almost the only colour.

Ink, paper, vermilion, print-green, print-red. That restraint is why the site looks expensive. But it also means the few accents you have are doing a lot of jobs — stamp text, hover state, severity marker, leader underline, button-equivalent links. The reader can't disambiguate "this is a section label" from "this is a clickable thing."

Action Reserve the vermilion for one role only — the severity / today's leader signal. Use ink-underline for links, and a faint gold (the --rule token, already in the system) for section labels.
